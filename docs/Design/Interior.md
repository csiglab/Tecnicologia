## Foundation

> What is the grounding of Interior Design Praxis? What grounds interior design evaluations?

> Interior design is grounded in a **model of the human**â€”their perception, cognition, affect, and activity patternsâ€”which allows us to reason systematically about space, form, and experience. By understanding how humans perceive, interact with, and are affected by their environments, we can infer a set of design principles that guide spatial, functional, and aesthetic decisions.

> These principles are applied to **optimize environments** so that they support human needs, enhance well-being, communicate meaning, and maintain coherence across spatial, sensory, temporal, and cultural dimensions.

> In essence, interior design praxis is a process of **translating human-centered understanding into structured form**, balancing **function, experience, and context**, while maintaining ethical, sustainable, and culturally resonant choices.

### Human Model

> What is the model or theory of a human that guides interior design? How are this models validaded?

> The "model" is not a formal model; rather, it is a set of observations about the human internal and interactional interface with the environment, intended to adapt the environment in ways that enhance performance according to a set of identified â€œrightâ€ or â€œwrongâ€ metrics.

The dimensions of the model are:

1. **Perceptual** â€“ How humans perceive visual, spatial, and sensory information, including light, color, texture, and form.
2. **Cognitive** â€“ How humans process information, navigate spaces, and understand functional layouts.
3. **Emotional** â€“ How environments influence mood, comfort, stress, and wellbeing.
4. **Behavioral** â€“ How humans interact with objects, furniture, and space; patterns of use and activity.
5. **Social (Cultural)** â€“ How humans interact socially, respect privacy, and perceive cultural meaning in spaces.
6. **Ergonomic** â€“ Physical comfort, accessibility, and support for diverse bodily needs.
7. **Adaptive** â€“ How humans respond to change, flexibility, and evolving uses of space.

## Design Meta Principle(s)

> A meta-principle operates at a higher level than stylistic or technical rules: it governs how principles are selected, prioritized, and interpreted by the design agent (human or artificial). It reflects the cognitive and systemic framework through which a designer perceives, evaluates, and structures a spatial environment.

> The meta-principles are grounded in three fundamental dimensions: the **Cognitiveâ€“Perceptual**, the **Functionalâ€“Systemic**, and the **Symbolic**.

> The fundamental problem of design lies in reconciling the multiplicity of human intentions, perceptual constraints, and systemic realities into a coherent whole. Design is not merely the act of shaping form or function, but the continuous negotiation between cognition and perceptionâ€”the way things are understood and the way they are seenâ€”between functionality and systemic orderâ€”the way things work and the way they interactâ€”and between the symbolic and the materialâ€”the meanings that things carry and the realities that sustain them. At its core, design seeks to make sense visible: to render the abstract intelligible, the functional graceful, and the complex harmonious. It is an art of synthesis, where logic meets intuition and where every choice, however minute, becomes a statement about how we wish the world to be organized, experienced, and remembered.

| **Meta-Principle**             | **Description**                                                                                   |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Spatial Coherence**          | Ensure consistency across spatial, visual, and functional elements to form an integrated whole.   |
| **Perceptual Harmony**         | Achieve visual balance and rhythm through proportion, contrast, and controlled variation.         |
| **Cognitive Economy**          | Reduce cognitive load by simplifying layout and enhancing legibility and intuitive use.           |
| **Functional Integration**     | Align spatial and material design with patterns of activity, comfort, and efficiency.             |
| **Environmental Adaptation**   | Adjust design to local environmental, climatic, and cultural conditions for optimal performance.  |
| **Temporal Modularity**        | Enable configurations and components to evolve or adapt over time without losing coherence.       |
| **Energy & Flow Optimization** | Maintain fluid, efficient circulation and functional flow of movement, light, and energy.         |
| **Symbolic Consistency**       | Preserve coherence between visual identity, cultural meaning, and functional expression.          |
| **Sensorial Integration**      | Harmonize sensory modalitiesâ€”sight, sound, touch, and lightâ€”into a unified perceptual experience. |
| **Ethical Materiality**        | Ensure material and process choices are sustainable, durable, and socially responsible.           |

## Pitfalls

> Common failure patterns that undermine spatial coherence or functionality.

| **Category**   | **Pitfall(s)**     | **Description**                                               |
| -------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Functionality  | Overdesign         | Excessive aesthetic intervention compromises usability.       |
| Aesthetics     | Style Incoherence  | Mixing visual languages without hierarchy or rationale.       |
| Lighting       | Flat Illumination  | Uniform lighting that eliminates depth and atmosphere.        |
| Acoustics      | Ignored Absorption | Hard surfaces amplify noise and reduce comfort.               |
| Scale          | Oversizing         | Furniture or fixtures overwhelm spatial proportions.          |
| Sustainability | Material Waste     | Poor selection leads to short lifespan or environmental cost. |
| Ergonomics     | Misalignment       | Furniture height or spacing incompatible with human posture.  |

## Praxis

> Actionable guidance to translate design principles into effective spatial environments.

### Structure

> How to structure the praxis of interior design?

| **Phase**                      | **Objective**                                           | **Key Activities**                                                                             | **Deliverables**                                         |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **1. Discovery**               | Understand needs, context, and constraints.             | - Client interviews<br>- Site analysis<br>- Space measurement<br>- Budget definition           | - Design brief<br>- Site report<br>- Mood references               |
| **2. Conceptualization**       | Generate spatial and aesthetic direction.               | - Define concept statement<br>- Develop mood boards<br>- Explore layout ideas                  | - Concept sketches<br>- Material & color direction<br>- Mood board |
| **3. Spatial Planning**        | Organize space based on function and flow.              | - Test furniture layouts<br>- Define zones and circulation paths<br>- Validate ergonomics      | - Floor plans<br>- Zoning diagrams<br>- Circulation maps           |
| **4. Design Development**      | Translate concept into detailed design logic.           | - Select materials, finishes, and lighting<br>- Refine proportions and compositions            | - Detailed drawings<br>- Material palette<br>- 3D models           |
| **5. Technical Documentation** | Formalize design for execution.                         | - Draft technical plans (electrical, plumbing, lighting)<br>- Specify furnishings and fittings | - Construction documents<br>- Specification sheets                 |
| **6. Budgeting & Procurement** | Align design with financial and logistical realities.   | - Cost estimation<br>- Vendor selection<br>- Procurement scheduling                            | - Bill of quantities<br>- Purchase orders<br>- Material samples    |
| **7. Implementation**          | Coordinate and supervise construction and installation. | - Site supervision<br>- Quality control<br>- Problem-solving during execution                  | - As-built records<br>- Site reports                               |
| **8. Styling & Finalization**  | Integrate final aesthetic elements.                     | - Furniture placement<br>- Accessorizing<br>- Lighting adjustment                              | - Completed space<br>- Photographic documentation                  |
| **9. Evaluation**              | Assess performance and satisfaction.                    | - Post-occupancy evaluation<br>- Client feedback<br>- Maintenance planning                     | - Evaluation report<br>- Maintenance guide                         |

### Advice

| **Category**           | **Advice**                               | **Description**                                                                            |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Space Planning**     | **Start with function**                  | Identify how each area will be used before deciding on layout or style.                    |
| **Space Planning**     | **Maintain clear circulation**           | Leave at least 90 cm of walking space between furniture and walls.                         |
| **Proportion & Scale** | **Match furniture to room size**         | Avoid oversized pieces in small rooms; scale elements to spatial volume.                   |
| **Lighting**           | **Layer your lighting**                  | Combine ambient, task, and accent lighting for flexibility and depth.                      |
| **Lighting**           | **Use warm light for comfort zones**     | Softer, warm-toned lighting enhances relaxation and intimacy.                              |
| **Color & Material**   | **Use color to shape perception**        | Light colors expand space; darker tones create intimacy and focus.                         |
| **Color & Material**   | **Limit the palette**                    | Stick to 3â€“4 dominant hues to maintain visual coherence.                                   |
| **Texture & Surface**  | **Mix textures subtly**                  | Combine matte and glossy, soft and hard surfaces for sensory balance.                      |
| **Furnishing**         | **Prioritize comfort and accessibility** | Always test seating and ensure furniture supports daily activity patterns.                 |
| **Storage**            | **Integrate storage into design**        | Conceal storage in built-ins or use multi-functional furniture to reduce clutter.          |
| **Acoustics**          | **Use soft materials strategically**     | Rugs, curtains, and upholstery absorb sound and reduce echo.                               |
| **Visual Composition** | **Create focal points**                  | Anchor each room with one dominant elementâ€”art, light fixture, or furniture piece.         |
| **Flow & Continuity**  | **Repeat motifs and materials**          | Use recurring elements (colors, trims, finishes) to unify different spaces.                |
| **Sustainability**     | **Prefer durable materials**             | Invest in quality items that age well rather than disposable dÃ©cor.                        |
| **Adaptability**       | **Design for change**                    | Choose modular or movable elements that can adapt to new needs or configurations.          |
| **Budgeting**          | **Invest in long-term impact**           | Spend more on key items (lighting, flooring, main furniture) and economize on accessories. |
| **Personalization**    | **Express identity through detail**      | Use art, textiles, or objects that reflect the inhabitantâ€™s story and taste.               |
